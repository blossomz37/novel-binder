### 2.4 Climate Fiction / Eco-SF

- **Core reader expectation**: By 2025, 3.5% of all published books fall into climate fiction categories. The genre has shifted from catastrophist to resilience-forward: "more hopeful than hopeless" is the dominant 2024–2025 mode. Readers want "emotional relief and new perspectives," not just warnings. [SRC] [RECENT] [WriteStats](https://writestats.com/climate-fiction-is-booming-in-2025-what-it-means-for-todays-authors/)
- **Character tropes**: (1) The local stayer — refuses evacuation, adapts in place, builds community in ruin (Lily Brooks-Dalton's Wanda, born during a hurricane); (2) The scientist-activist caught between data and advocacy; (3) The climate refugee carrying cultural memory; (4) The policy insider who must betray colleagues to act (*Ministry for the Future*); (5) The child born into the new normal who doesn't mourn the old world
- **Plot tropes**: (1) The incremental disappearance — community or coastline vanishes chapter by chapter; (2) The policy thriller — institutional maneuvering, legislation, coalition-building as plot engine (Robinson's *Ministry for the Future*); (3) Found family in ruins — survivors build new community norms; (4) The ecological heist — stealing resources from the wealthy to sustain the commons; (5) The flashback-forward structure showing both the cause and the consequence
- **Theme tropes**: (1) The commons vs. enclosure — who owns resources, land, and air in crisis; (2) Technology as tool vs. technology as salvation; (3) Intergenerational responsibility and guilt; (4) The right to remain — versus flight and displacement; (5) Complicity of comfortable classes
- **Tonal range**: Dystopian/grim pole (Bacigalupi, El Akkad); hopeful/adaptation pole (Mandel, Doctorow, solarpunk); policy/wonk pole (Robinson's near-reportage density)
- **Adjacent niches**: Solarpunk (hopeful subgenre; solarpunk is post-transition while cli-fi can be mid-crisis); Dystopian/post-collapse (cli-fi shades into dystopia when collapse is complete); Literary SF (most prestigious cli-fi is reviewed as literary fiction)
- **Key divergence points**: Solarpunk requires ecological/political commitment AND positive action; cli-fi can be purely catastrophist. Dystopia can be caused by anything; cli-fi is climate-causally specific.
- **Recent market examples**: *Playground* (Richard Powers, Norton, 2024) — #2 in 1,600-reader 2024 survey; *All the Water in the World* (Eiren Caffall, 2025) — NYPL recommended; *The Light Pirate* (Lily Brooks-Dalton) — Duke Libraries Earth Month 2026; *2084* (Ackerman & Stavridis, Penguin, May 2026); *Private Rites* (Julia Armfield, 2025) — Arthur C. Clarke Award shortlist 2025
- **Demand vs. supply assessment**: Agent and publisher appetite explicitly "high" in 2025. Climate Fiction Prize (first awarded 2025) formally legitimizes the category. The gap is in plot-driven cli-fi that is neither utopian travelogue nor pure doom.
