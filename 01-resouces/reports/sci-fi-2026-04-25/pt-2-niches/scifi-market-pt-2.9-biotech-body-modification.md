### 2.9 Biotech / Body Modification

- **Core reader expectation**: "The way people learn to adopt and embrace change, about the ways characters in SF often find themselves and reaffirm themselves through the processes of transformation." Body modification is identity transformation made literal. [SRC] [READER] [BookTrib](https://booktrib.com/2024/08/22/5-speculative-and-science-fiction-novels-to-lose-your-body-in/)
- **Character tropes**: (1) The modified body as political statement — modification as resistance, assimilation, or both; (2) The alien-infected or alien-modified human — body as contact zone with non-human biology; (3) The bioengineered worker/soldier — modification imposed by corporate or military power; (4) The killer cyborg seeking transformation (*The Years Shall Run Like Rabbits*, Ben Berman Ghan, 2024); (5) The trans/queer body modifier whose modification is both personal and political (Rivers Solomon, Premee Mohamed)
- **Plot tropes**: (1) The alien ecosystem that rewrites the human body involuntarily — accept or resist; (2) The corporation that patents living bodies/pathogens — biocapitalism as antagonist (*Autonomous*, Newitz); (3) The pandemic-like spread of biological modification (Thompson's alien dome in Rosewater heals and transforms all nearby); (4) The de-extinction as contact event (*Tusks of Extinction*, Nayler); (5) The corporate trial of a modified person — does the body still belong to them?
- **Theme tropes**: (1) Bodily autonomy as political frontier — who controls what can be done to a body; (2) The post-human as new normal — communities of modified humans developing distinct culture; (3) Gender and sexuality as modifiable parameters; (4) Colonialism mirrored in alien modification — external force rewriting indigenous body; (5) The question of what makes a body still "yours"
- **Tonal range**: Afro/Global-South biopunk (Tade Thompson's Rosewater — postcolonial, Nigerian urban reality); literary-quiet (*Never Let Me Go* — interiority over spectacle); action-horror (alien biology as threat in *Alien Clay*); queer/trans body horror/celebration (Rivers Solomon, Premee Mohamed)
- **Adjacent niches**: Cyberpunk (biopunk is wetware; cyberpunk is hardware; boundaries collapse in biocyberpunk); First-contact (when alien modification is the contact event); Literary SF (most prestigious biopunk is reviewed as literary)
- **Key divergence points**: Cyberpunk modifies with hardware; biopunk modifies with biology. The experiential difference is that biopunk is about what the body becomes, while cyberpunk is about what the augmented body can do.
- **Recent market examples**: *Alien Clay* (Tchaikovsky, Orbit, 2024) — Hugo nominee 2025; *The Years Shall Run Like Rabbits* (Ben Berman Ghan, Wolsak & Wynn, 2024); *The Tusks of Extinction* (Nayler, Tordotcom, 2024) — Hugo winner 2025; *Private Rites* (Julia Armfield, 2025) — Clarke Award shortlist 2025 (cli-fi/biopunk); *In Ascension* (Martin MacInnes, 2024) — Clarke Award winner 2024 (literary-SF/biopunk/cli-fi)
- **Demand vs. supply assessment**: Publisher trend reports name biopunk as expected 2025–2026 growth area alongside cli-fi and hopepunk. Very few major dedicated biopunk titles from large trad publishers — the subgenre largely lives inside first-contact or literary SF rather than as standalone marketing category.
