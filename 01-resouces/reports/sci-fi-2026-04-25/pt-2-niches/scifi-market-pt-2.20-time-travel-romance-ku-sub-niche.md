### 2.20 Time-Travel Romance (KU Sub-Niche)

- **Core reader expectation**: Time-travel romance is distinct from time-travel SF: the HEA/HFN (happily ever after/happily for now) is required. The temporal mechanics serve the romance; the romance does not serve the SF premise. Most involve a modern protagonist transported to a historical period (Scottish Highlands, Regency England, medieval Scotland) — combining the appeal of both historical romance and SF. [SRC]
- **Character tropes**: (1) The accidental time-traveler — protagonist did not choose to travel; must adapt; (2) The Highland/historical hero — physically imposing, honor-bound, initially baffled by modern references; (3) The modern woman with skills that create friction or solve problems in the historical period; (4) The antagonist who threatens the anachronistic romance from period-appropriate social norms; (5) The secondary character who serves as the hero's voice in explaining the historical world
- **Plot tropes**: (1) Fish-out-of-water adaptation — protagonist learning historical norms while bringing modern perspective; (2) The return question — will she have to leave the time period? Can HEA be sustained across time?; (3) The world-historical event as romantic backdrop — love story set against actual historical crisis; (4) The time mechanism as metaphor — what you have to leave behind to find what you need; (5) The parallel timeline in which the choice not made is briefly glimpsed
- **Theme tropes**: (1) The past as refuge from modernity — implicit critique of contemporary alienation; (2) Female agency in constrained historical context — modern protagonist pushes against historical gender norms; (3) The authentic relationship uncomplicated by contemporary irony; (4) The simplicity of clear moral codes as escapism; (5) The question of whether love can transcend time as the genre's literally enacted metaphor
- **Tonal range**: Pure romance (comedy-of-misunderstanding, warm, low-stakes outside the relationship); adventure-romance (actual historical dangers as subplot); paranormal-romance adjacent (stone circles, magical objects, Highland witches as mechanism)
- **Adjacent niches**: SFR (both KU-dominant romance-forward SF; settings differ — historical period vs. alien world); Romantasy (historical time-travel romance overlaps with historical fantasy romance aesthetics); Literary SF (*The Ministry of Time* is the literary exception; most KU time-travel romance is genre-commercial)
- **Key divergence points**: SFR centers alien/future settings; time-travel romance centers historical settings. The SF mechanism in time-travel romance is almost always magic-adjacent (stones, portals, ancient objects) rather than technological. Literary time-travel SF (*The Ministry of Time*, *Sea of Tranquility*) foregrounds the SF premise as philosophical investigation; KU time-travel romance uses it as setup.
- **Recent market examples**: *The Ministry of Time* (Kaliane Bradley, Doubleday, 2024) — #1 in 1,600-reader 2024 SF poll; trad-published literary time-travel romance hybrid; KU time-travel romance — numerous serialized series, exact titles not surfaced in research; Connie Willis's *Blackout*/*All Clear* — WWII time-travel, not romance-primary but romance-inclusive, still recommended 2025
- **Demand vs. supply assessment**: Subgenre benefits from the enormous infrastructure of historical romance readership. *The Ministry of Time*'s mainstream success demonstrates appetite for upmarket time-travel SF that is not pure genre-commercial.
