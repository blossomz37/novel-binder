# Story Bible Contents Checklist

A practical story bible usually grows from big-picture idea to detailed reference. Here’s a clean development order an author would commonly use.

## Development order

1. Premise — the core concept in 1–3 sentences: who the story is about, what disrupts their life, and why it matters.

2. Genre and audience — genre, age category, tone, and the kind of reading experience you’re aiming to deliver.

3. Story promise — the emotional, thematic, and commercial expectations the book should fulfill.

4. Theme and central question — what the story is really exploring underneath the plot.

5. Logline — a sharper one-sentence version of the premise.

6. Rough plot shape — beginning, middle, end; the broad arc before scene-level planning.

7. Main protagonist profile — goal, need, flaw, wound, fear, misbelief, strengths, and change arc.

8. Main antagonist or opposing force — what stands in the protagonist’s way and why that force is credible.

9. Core supporting cast — allies, rivals, love interests, mentors, foils, and each person’s function in the story.

10. Relationship map — how major characters connect, conflict, depend on, or hide things from one another.

11. Setting basics — place, time period, social environment, and any key location constraints.

12. Worldbuilding rules — political, cultural, technological, magical, scientific, economic, or social systems that govern the story.

13. Stakes — what can be lost personally, relationally, socially, and externally.

14. Point of view plan — whose heads we enter, in what tense, and under what limitations.

15. Plot structure beats — inciting incident, first turning point, midpoint, crisis, climax, resolution, or whichever structure model you use.

16. Character arcs — how each major character changes, resists change, or fails to change.

17. Scene list or chapter list — the working sequence of events.

18. Subplots — secondary threads, who carries them, and where they intersect the main plot.

19. Timeline — calendar, chronology, durations, travel time, ages, seasons, and cause-effect order.

20. Locations dossier — recurring places with sensory details, layout, and story function.

21. Research notes — factual references, source reminders, terminology, and reality checks.

22. Lore and continuity rules — naming conventions, invented terms, system rules, backstory facts, and what cannot be contradicted.

23. Symbolism and motifs — repeated images, objects, themes, phrases, or parallels you want to use intentionally.

24. Scene-level continuity tracker — injuries, clothing, weather, objects, secrets known, and unresolved threads.

25. Voice notes — diction, dialogue style, narrative texture, taboo phrases, and stylistic guardrails.

26. Revision notes — problems discovered in drafting, fixes to carry forward, and decisions made canon.
