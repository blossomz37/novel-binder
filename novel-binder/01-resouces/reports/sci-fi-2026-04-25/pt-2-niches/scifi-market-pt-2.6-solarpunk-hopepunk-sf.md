### 2.6 Solarpunk / Hopepunk SF

- **Core reader expectation**: Explicitly defined in opposition to cyberpunk and dystopia. Core aesthetic: eco-conscious design, community-centered living, technological advancement compatible with sustainability. Key tenet: "you don't need to wait for new technology — just political will." [SRC] [READER] [Mother Jones](https://www.motherjones.com/environment/2026/02/solarpunk-sci-fi-books-literary-genres-climate-change-optimism-technology/)
- **Character tropes**: (1) The community organizer/local leader who works through collective action rather than individual heroism; (2) The "climate refugee settler" who builds new community in disaster-reshaped landscape; (3) The conservative antagonist — MAGA-analogue or neoliberal holdout resisting the commons; (4) The engineer whose practical problem-solving is the heroic action; (5) The child of the transition who takes the new world for granted
- **Plot tropes**: (1) The community defense against reactionary backlash; (2) The practical problem-solving arc — engineering, social organizing, mutual aid as plot action; (3) Robot/AI as community partner (Chambers's Monk and Robot: robots step back, humans figure out what they want); (4) The political negotiation that secures the commons; (5) The incursion of the old world and the decision whether to absorb or repel it
- **Theme tropes**: (1) Anti-capitalism as prerequisite — "easier to imagine the end of the world than the end of capitalism; solarpunk flips that"; (2) Mutual aid and commons governance over individual heroism; (3) Technology as already available — solar, wind, 3D printing, passive cooling; (4) The refusal of despair as political act; (5) Joy as resistance
- **Tonal range**: Cozy/gentle (Becky Chambers's Monk and Robot — low stakes, community warmth); activist/confrontational (Doctorow's *The Lost Cause* — MAGA relatives, organized resistance); literary/ecological (Kim Stanley Robinson)
- **Adjacent niches**: Cli-fi (solarpunk is the hopeful subgenre of cli-fi; diverges in tone and temporal placement — post-transition vs. mid-crisis); Cozy SF (significant overlap with Chambers but solarpunk requires ecological/political commitment); Dystopia (defined as solarpunk's opposite)
- **Key divergence points**: Solarpunk's acknowledged weakness is "treacly societies too well-adjusted to be believable." The most compelling works retain human conflict and difficulty even after the transition. Cozy SF has no mandatory politics; solarpunk does.
- **Recent market examples**: *A Psalm for the Wild-Built* / *A Prayer for the Crown-Shy* (Becky Chambers, Tordotcom, 2021/2022 — still the canonical reference texts in 2024–2025); *The Lost Cause* (Cory Doctorow, Tor, 2023, major review cycle 2024); *Automatic Noodle* (Annalee Newitz, 2025) — Andrew Liptak's best-of-2025
- **Demand vs. supply assessment**: Community aesthetic activity (subreddits, Tumblrs, Substacks, mood boards) significantly exceeds literary output — a classic [GAP] signal. Publisher forecasts name "notable rise in hopepunk" as anticipated 2025 expansion. Plot-driven solarpunk that is thriller-shaped, not utopian travelogue, is undersupplied.
