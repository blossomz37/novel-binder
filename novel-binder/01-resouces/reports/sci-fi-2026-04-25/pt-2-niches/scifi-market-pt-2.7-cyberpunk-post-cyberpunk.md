### 2.7 Cyberpunk / Post-Cyberpunk

- **Core reader expectation**: Marginalized protagonists (hackers, street hustlers, mercenaries), high tech/low life, corporate power as villain, blurred human/machine boundary, street-level perspective, distrust of technological progress as inherently beneficial. [SRC] [J. Paul Roe](https://jpaulroe.com/cyberpunk-books-to-read-2025/)
- **Character tropes**: (1) The console cowboy/hacker-for-hire whose technical virtuosity is their only social capital; (2) The renegade corporate agent/fixer who serves the system while being destroyed by it; (3) The woman with claws — cyberpunk heroines who deploy violence instrumentally (Grimdark Magazine 2025); (4) The augmented body-for-hire who questions what remains of selfhood; (5) The Asian diaspora protagonist who inhabits and critiques the genre's Japanese-inflected aesthetics (Chloe Gong pattern, 2025 NYT roundup)
- **Plot tropes**: (1) The heist/extraction job that reveals a larger corporate conspiracy; (2) Identity theft and consciousness transfer — which "self" is authentic; (3) The AI gone rogue/more human than humans; (4) The data trail that incriminates everyone; (5) The body upgrade that costs personhood
- **Theme tropes**: (1) Mind-body dualism as class issue — who can afford to transfer consciousness; (2) Corporate sovereignty replacing national governance — echoes in tech-billionaire sovereign states (more relevant in 2025 than ever); (3) Asian diaspora aesthetics critiquing the genre's own cultural assumptions; (4) Surveillance capitalism as lifestyle, not crime; (5) The obsolescent body in a world of upgrades
- **Tonal range**: Classic gritty noir (Gibson, Sterling, Morgan); post-cyberpunk evolution (biotech, pharmaceutical politics, AI rights — Newitz's *Autonomous*); eco-cyberpunk (Bacigalupi's *The Water Knife* — water as scarce resource); cyberpunk-samurai space opera (Fonda Lee's *The Last Contract of Isako*, 2025)
- **Adjacent niches**: Biotech SF (post-cyberpunk increasingly merges with biopunk — hardware vs. wetware); Near-future techno-thriller (shares corporate conspiracy and technological anxiety; techno-thriller is more resolution-oriented); Dystopia (cyberpunk is usually a functional unjust society, not collapsed)
- **Key divergence points**: Near-future techno-thriller protagonists usually succeed; cyberpunk is often nihilistic. Biotech SF is wetware (biology); cyberpunk is hardware (tech implants) — boundaries collapse in biocyberpunk hybrids. Dystopia implies collapse; cyberpunk is "business as usual" under corporate control.
- **Recent market examples**: *The Escher Man* (T.R. Napper, 2024) — called "the future of cyberpunk" by Grimdark Magazine; *Local Heavens* (KM Fajardo, 2025) — Gatsby-meets-cyberpunk, 2075 NYC; *The Last Contract of Isako* (Fonda Lee, Winter 2025); Richard Morgan reissues (*Altered Carbon* TV cycle still driving onboarding)
- **Demand vs. supply assessment**: 2025 resurgence driven by real-world parallels (AI, billionaire sovereignty, surveillance capitalism). The [GAP] is in cyberpunk that engages actual 2025+ technology (LLMs, on-device AI, surveillance capitalism, algorithmic labor management) rather than recycling 1980s aesthetic.
