### 2.14 Adult YA / Crossover SF

- **Core reader expectation**: Adults reading YA is now so dominant that two new YA imprints were launched 2025–2026 specifically for ages 13–16 because adults had taken over existing YA content. YA SF readers expect a teenage protagonist making consequential choices in a world that has failed them; the adult system is usually the antagonist. [SRC] [PUBLISHER] [Miss Demeanors](https://www.missdemeanors.com/3-publishing-trends-11-genre-trends-for-2025-2026/)
- **Character tropes**: (1) The chosen-but-reluctant protagonist who refuses the chosen-one frame (Haymitch in *Sunrise on the Reaping*: "unsuspecting, kind-hearted teen boy, clever but not a classic hero"); (2) The mentor who is actually compromised — adults who cannot be trusted; (3) The queer teen protagonist whose identity is intertwined with political resistance (Andrew Joseph White's pattern); (4) The child soldier who develops moral agency despite the system; (5) The ordinary teen who is extraordinary only by circumstance
- **Plot tropes**: (1) The competition-as-control system — protagonist thrust into legibility-generating mechanism that reveals society's values; (2) The revolution that costs everything — Mockingjay template; (3) The prequel that expands political worldbuilding (*Sunrise on the Reaping*: 50th Hunger Games, 24 years before original); (4) The surveillance system the teen must learn to navigate without being legible; (5) The new-adult pivot — crossing the genre boundary as the series matures with its reader
- **Theme tropes**: (1) Political manipulation and propaganda — "political manipulation, the power of propaganda, and the complexities of societal control" (*Sunrise on the Reaping*); (2) The enclosure of the commons — who owns public resources; (3) Identity formation under surveillance — where you cannot be who you are; (4) The price of being right — winning the argument costs everything; (5) Collective action vs. individualist heroism
- **Tonal range**: Mainstream crossover (Collins — accessible prose, high stakes, political depth, maximum market penetration); dark/queer/transgressive (Andrew Joseph White — body horror, hive transformation, pushing limits); hopeful/identity-forward (non-binary protagonists, Indigenous-centered narratives, disability-positive)
- **Adjacent niches**: Adult dystopia (YA has faster pacing, less moral ambiguity, identity formation central; adult can be unresolved); Romantasy YA (YA SF increasingly romance-inflected; *Shatter Me* revival, *Iron Widow* demonstrate SF + romance crossover); Adult crossover SF (literary adult SF doesn't behave like YA despite readership overlap)
- **Key divergence points**: YA dystopia presumes the system can be changed; adult dystopia often does not. Adult crossover SF (*The Ministry of Time*, *Playground*) is driven by premise and prose, not protagonist age and identity formation.
- **Recent market examples**: *Sunrise on the Reaping* (Collins, Scholastic, March 2025) — 1.5M copies first week; *Watch Me* (Shatter Me: The New Republic #1, Tahereh Mafi, 2025) — Goodreads Choice Award YA nominee; *Heavenly Tyrant* (Iron Widow #2, Xiran Jay Zhao, 2025); *You Weren't Meant to be Human* (Andrew Joseph White, Sept 2025); *A Fate So Cold* (Chloe Gong, Tor Teen, Nov 2025)
- **Demand vs. supply assessment**: YA SF "still high in demand." *Sunrise on the Reaping*'s extraordinary first-week sales (tripling prior series records) confirm sustained appetite for Hunger Games-style political dystopian YA SF at scale. Agents and editors want it; the adults-reading-YA phenomenon is reshaping it. [GAP] for YA-adult crossover SF without doomerism.
