### 2.10 Near-Future Techno-Thriller SF

- **Core reader expectation**: Plausible near-future extrapolation of current technology combined with thriller pacing — high stakes, fast chapters, protagonist-in-danger. Strong crossover appeal: regular appearances on general bestseller lists. Blake Crouch, Daniel Suarez, Eliot Peper attract readers who don't self-identify as SF readers. [SRC] [SALES]
- **Character tropes**: (1) The ordinary expert suddenly entangled in extraordinary technological conspiracy — physicist, programmer, or bioengineer rather than spy or soldier; (2) The whistleblower reluctantly going public — institutional insider who becomes the threat; (3) The tech-billionaire antagonist — sovereign tech founder operating outside law; (4) The government insider playing both sides; (5) The scientist who designed the weapon and must destroy it
- **Plot tropes**: (1) "What if this technology already existed" reveal — thriller momentum built on suppressed tech discovery; (2) Race against institutional shutdown — protagonist must prove the technology's threat before elimination; (3) Reality destabilization — multiverse, simulated reality, memory manipulation as thriller premise; (4) The double-edged breakthrough that solves one problem and creates existential risk; (5) The three-country race to control the technology
- **Theme tropes**: (1) Corporate sovereignty and state collapse — technology companies replacing governments; (2) Privacy as frontier — surveillance tech, algorithmic control, data ownership; (3) The democratization of destructive capability — any sufficiently motivated individual can now cause mass harm; (4) Military-tech escalation without military control; (5) The ethics of suppressing dangerous knowledge vs. the rights of free science
- **Tonal range**: High-concept pop SF (Blake Crouch — rapid-fire, cinematic, accessible); policy-wonk SF-thriller (P.W. Singer's *Burn-In*, Suarez — technically rigorous, slower); literary crossover (Eliot Peper — character interiority alongside tech premises)
- **Adjacent niches**: Military SF (techno-thriller is civilian/systemic; mil-SF is unit-level); Cyberpunk (techno-thriller is more resolution-oriented and less nihilistic); Literary SF (techno-thrillers are explicitly genre-commercial; rarely overlap)
- **Key divergence points**: Techno-thrillers end with resolution (protagonist usually succeeds, or at least the threat is neutralized). Cyberpunk ends in ambiguity or defeat. Military SF is squad-embedded; techno-thriller is institutional/systemic.
- **Recent market examples**: *2054* (Ackerman & Stavridis, Penguin, 2024); *2084* (Ackerman & Stavridis, Penguin, May 2026); *Dissolution* (Nicholas Binge, 2025) — "SF techno thriller like *Inception* meets *Eternal Sunshine*" (5-star T.R. Napper); *The Man Who Saw Seconds* (Alexander Boldizar) — Locus Award winner 2025 (public vote), crossover mainstream success
- **Demand vs. supply assessment**: Traditional thriller publishers (Penguin, Random House) are dominant — not genre imprints. The niche produces consistent mainstream crossover hits, suggesting significant latent demand from general fiction readers willing to engage SF when thriller-packaged.
